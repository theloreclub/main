# The Lore Club: Product and Acquisition Strategy

Research date: June 15, 2026

## Executive recommendation

The Lore Club should not position itself as a marketplace for downloadable
itineraries. Expedia and Airbnb now offer creator recommendations, AI trip
planning, shared itineraries, activities, and services. A generic itinerary
product is therefore easy for them to reproduce.

The stronger position is:

> The marketplace and operating system for occasion-based group trips, powered
> by plans from people who actually took them.

Start with trips where planning is unusually painful and the organizer is
highly motivated:

- Bachelorettes and bachelor trips
- Birthday and friends' weekends
- Family reunions and multigenerational trips
- Company off-sites
- Weddings and destination events

The acquisition-worthy asset is not the itinerary document. It is the
combination of:

1. A trusted creator network with differentiated, reusable trip supply.
2. Structured data about what real groups planned, changed, booked, spent, and
   enjoyed.
3. High-intent demand that converts into lodging, activities, transport, food,
   and services.
4. Group-planning workflows that keep The Lore Club in use before, during, and
   after a trip.

This creates something Expedia or Airbnb could integrate faster than it could
rebuild: an active marketplace plus a proprietary "occasion travel graph."

## What Teachers Pay Teachers actually built

Teachers Pay Teachers (TPT) is a vertical marketplace for educator-created
content. Its current public figures include more than 7 million educators,
7 million teacher-created lessons, and 1 billion resource downloads. TPT also
reports more than $2 billion paid to sellers.

Its business model has several layers:

### Marketplace take rate and seller subscriptions

TPT offers two seller tiers:

- Basic: $29 one-time fee, 55% seller payout, and a $0.30 transaction fee per
  resource.
- Premium: $59.95 per year, 80% seller payout, and a $0.15 fee only on orders
  below $3.

The premium tier combines a better economic rate with marketing and analytics
tools. That gives TPT recurring subscription revenue while rewarding serious
sellers for investing in the marketplace.

### Deep vertical discovery

TPT's catalog is organized around the specific attributes teachers use to make
decisions: grade, subject, resource type, topic, standards, season, and use
case. This turns an enormous catalog into useful long-tail search inventory and
supports search-engine acquisition.

### Creator businesses, not isolated uploads

Sellers receive storefronts, sales and conversion data, marketing tools,
follower communication, promotions, and the ability to revise resources.
Buyers can follow sellers and receive new-product notifications.

### Merchandising and repeat-purchase loops

TPT supports:

- Free resources as sampling and acquisition tools
- Wish lists
- Bundles that update when included resources change
- Seller discounts and marketplace sales
- Additional licenses
- Reviews
- Credits for buyers who review purchases

Credits turn post-purchase feedback into both marketplace trust and another
purchase.

### A usage layer beyond the file

Easel by TPT turns some marketplace products into interactive, online
activities and assessments. TPT also exposes usage data such as opens,
assignments, and reach.

This is strategically important: TPT is not only where a resource is bought.
It is also where the resource can be used and measured.

### Institutional purchasing

TPT School Express lets administrators preload funds, allocate individual or
shared budgets, set purchasing rules, and monitor activity. Teachers can buy
immediately without reimbursement or per-purchase approval.

This converts individual demand into a controlled B2B purchasing channel.

### Why TPT was acquired

IXL Learning acquired TPT in March 2023. IXL emphasized TPT's large catalog,
educator expertise, trust, community, and digital instruction tools.

The lesson for The Lore Club is direct: a strategic buyer will care more about
unique supply, trusted relationships, structured usage data, and adjacency to
its core transactions than about a polished collection of static files.

## The wedge: occasion-based group travel

The Lore Club should remain narrow until it owns a recognizable category.
Trying to cover every destination and traveler immediately would put it in
direct competition with much larger general-purpose platforms.

Group occasion travel is attractive because:

- The organizer has a concrete deadline and a high cost of failure.
- The same plan can be reused by many similar groups.
- Group size creates meaningful transaction value.
- Coordination, preferences, budgets, and payments create recurring product
  use beyond inspiration.
- The trip naturally includes lodging, activities, transport, restaurants,
  services, and event vendors.
- Participants can become future organizers, creating a built-in referral loop.

The initial promise should be outcome-oriented:

> Start with a trip that already worked, customize it for your group, and book
> it together.

## Product principles

### 1. Sell a working trip, not a PDF

Every purchase should create an editable Trip Workspace containing:

- Day-by-day schedule
- Interactive map and travel times
- Group invitations and roles
- Polls and voting
- Shared notes
- Budget per person and total budget
- Expense allocation and payment status
- Reservation and confirmation storage
- Calendar export
- Bookable lodging, activities, transport, and services
- Alternatives when a recommendation is unavailable
- Offline/mobile access during the trip

This is The Lore Club's equivalent of TPT's Easel layer.

### 2. Structure every itinerary

The platform should store itinerary data as reusable entities rather than one
text or JSON blob.

Core attributes should include:

- Destination and neighborhood
- Occasion and traveler type
- Group-size range
- Duration
- Estimated total and per-person budget
- Season and weather suitability
- Pace and nightlife level
- Child, pet, mobility, dietary, and alcohol considerations
- Venue, activity, lodging, restaurant, transport, and service records
- Geographic coordinates and travel time
- Booking URL, affiliate source, price range, and availability status
- Plan version, last verified date, and creator notes

The structured graph is necessary for search, recommendations, freshness,
booking attribution, AI customization, and strategic value.

### 3. Prove that the plan worked

Introduce a "Trip Proven" trust system:

- Creator verifies that they took the trip.
- Buyers can review only after purchase and trip activation.
- Reviews capture group type, dates, spend accuracy, pace, and what changed.
- Completed-trip participants can confirm or challenge recommendations.
- Listings show last verified date and freshness warnings.
- Ranking favors recent, completed, high-satisfaction trips rather than raw
  clicks.

The marketplace should distinguish:

- Proven trip
- Local expert plan
- Professional planner plan
- Community-curated draft

### 4. Make supply creation radically easy

Creators should be able to import:

- PDF, Word, spreadsheet, or plain-text itinerary
- Google Docs
- Google Maps saved places
- Calendar events
- Confirmation emails
- Notes or chat exports
- A public travel post or creator video, when rights permit

AI should extract structured days, places, times, budgets, and booking links.
The creator then reviews the result in an editor before publishing.

AI is an onboarding and customization tool, not the primary product. The source
of truth remains human experience plus verified marketplace outcomes.

### 5. Give creators a business

Creator functionality should include:

- Branded storefront and public profile
- Expertise tags and verified trip history
- Followers and update notifications
- Free samples
- Collections and bundles
- Coupons and referral links
- Versioned product updates
- Buyer questions with response-time indicators
- Analytics for impressions, listing conversion, trip activation, booking
  clicks, attributed bookings, revenue, reviews, refunds, and repeat buyers
- Payout ledger and tax onboarding

## Business model

Use multiple revenue streams, but add them in sequence.

### Phase 1: Marketplace revenue

- Keep the current 15% marketplace fee for plan purchases.
- Do not charge an upfront seller fee while supply is scarce.
- Use Stripe Connect so seller onboarding, application fees, refunds, and
  payouts are real and auditable.

### Phase 2: Booking and lead revenue

Monetize the larger trip, not only the $20-$50 plan:

- Lodging affiliate or partner commissions
- Activity and experience commissions
- Ground transport and car rental commissions
- Travel insurance
- Grocery, luggage, photography, beauty, chef, and event-service referrals
- Qualified vendor leads

Creators should share in attributed booking revenue. This aligns them with
fresh, bookable recommendations and makes The Lore Club more competitive with
Expedia's creator program.

### Phase 3: Creator Pro

After creators are earning, introduce an optional subscription in the spirit
of TPT Premium:

- Lower marketplace take rate
- Advanced analytics
- More AI imports and refresh checks
- Custom branding and domains
- Email/follower tools
- Team accounts
- Priority support
- Enhanced affiliate revenue share

An illustrative model is a free tier at a 15% plan fee and a paid tier around
$15-$25 per month at an 8%-10% plan fee. Pricing should be validated against
actual creator earnings before launch.

### Phase 4: Lore for Teams

Adapt the School Express idea for:

- Company off-sites
- Executive assistants
- Wedding and event planners
- Travel advisors
- Hotels and property managers
- Destination marketing organizations

Capabilities should include:

- Preloaded individual or shared budgets
- Spend policies and approved categories
- Team permissions
- Centralized billing and receipts
- Reusable company trip templates
- Reporting and traveler duty-of-care fields
- Curated private catalogs

Revenue can combine a platform subscription, per-trip fee, and booking
commissions.

### Later: Media and destination partnerships

Sponsored placements and destination campaigns can be valuable only after
organic ranking and disclosure rules are trusted. Paid content must never be
allowed to quietly replace outcome-based recommendations.

## Product roadmap

### Stage 0: Transaction and trust foundation

Complete this before marketplace growth.

1. Add server-side authentication and authorization to every protected route.
2. Add Supabase migrations, foreign keys, row-level security, and audit fields.
3. Fetch listing price and ownership on the server during checkout. Never
   accept price or product name from the browser.
4. Use Stripe Connect for seller onboarding, application fees, refunds, and
   payouts.
5. Require webhook signature verification in production and add event
   idempotency.
6. Create fulfillment records and a Trip Workspace after confirmed payment.
7. Enforce published, active, and purchasable listing states.
8. Restrict orders, trips, reviews, messages, analytics, and files by ownership.
9. Add moderation, IP reporting, refund, and creator-content policies.
10. Add error monitoring, product analytics, tests, backups, and data-retention
    controls.

### Stage 1: Desirable marketplace MVP

1. Structured listing editor and AI import review.
2. Occasion-focused search, filters, and landing pages.
3. Rich listing pages with preview schedule, map, inclusions, creator proof,
   freshness, verified reviews, and clear refund terms.
4. Functional purchase and instant Trip Workspace creation.
5. Editable itinerary, dates, map, calendar, budget, and group invitations.
6. Creator profiles, storefronts, and seller analytics.
7. Verified post-trip reviews and Lore Credits.
8. Mobile-first trip mode.

### Stage 2: Marketplace growth loops

1. Creator follows and product-update notifications.
2. Free samples, wish lists, bundles, and collections.
3. Referral links for creators, organizers, and invited travelers.
4. Group polls, voting, comments, and decision deadlines.
5. Price and availability refresh checks.
6. Creator response tools and limited post-purchase support.
7. Booking links with attribution and creator revenue sharing.
8. Ranking based on relevance, freshness, outcomes, and conversion quality.

### Stage 3: Transactional trip graph

1. Make itinerary items directly bookable where integrations allow.
2. Add substitution logic when a place is unavailable or unsuitable.
3. Build personalized cloning based on group profile, dates, budget, and pace.
4. Track planned versus actual spend and planned versus completed activities.
5. Expose partner APIs or widgets for hotels, travel advisors, and employers.
6. Launch Lore for Teams and private catalogs.

## Current codebase assessment

The repo contains the right conceptual primitives:

- Listings and creator pages
- Seller upload and pricing UI
- Stripe Checkout and a 15% platform-fee concept
- Orders and post-purchase trips
- Reviews and messaging
- Seller analytics
- A trip dashboard with schedule, vendors, and budget concepts

It is still a prototype rather than a functioning marketplace. The highest-risk
gaps are:

### Payments

`app/api/checkout/route.ts` accepts names and prices supplied by the client.
This lets a caller choose the amount charged. Checkout must load a published
listing and its canonical price from the database.

`app/api/webhook/stripe/route.ts` accepts unsigned JSON whenever the webhook
secret is absent, has no event-id idempotency, ignores database errors, and
records a seller payout without actually creating one.

### Authorization

Listing creation accepts a caller-supplied seller ID, and reviews, messages,
orders, trips, and analytics have no ownership checks. A service-role Supabase
client makes those omissions especially dangerous because it bypasses normal
row-level restrictions.

### Fulfillment

Payment does not unlock files, create a Trip Workspace, create messaging
access, send a receipt, or queue a payout. Those tasks remain a TODO in the
webhook.

### Product usability

The listing purchase button, trip sharing, and PDF export are not connected to
actions. Browse and home content are static. The trip dashboard is read-only,
and the upload flow stores a file without turning it into a product.

### Data and quality

There is no checked-in schema or migration history, no itinerary versioning,
no freshness model, no rights/moderation flow, and no event instrumentation.
The AI parser is currently a small text heuristic.

### Build health

The production build currently stops on a nullable `listings` result in
`app/api/seller/analytics/route.ts`. Next.js also warns that the `appDir`
experimental option is obsolete.

## The data moat

The durable product loop should be:

1. A creator imports a trip that really happened.
2. The Lore Club turns it into a structured, searchable plan.
3. A buyer purchases and clones it for a real group.
4. The group collaborates, changes items, and books.
5. The completed trip produces verified outcome, spend, substitution, and
   satisfaction data.
6. The platform improves ranking, estimates, recommendations, and creator
   analytics.
7. Better creator earnings attract more high-quality supply.

Generic AI can generate plausible itineraries. It cannot easily reproduce a
large corpus of consented, structured, completed-trip outcomes tied to creator
reputation and transaction behavior.

## Acquisition fit

### Expedia Group

Expedia is the clearest near-term strategic fit, but also a direct competitive
threat.

It already offers Travel Shops where creators curate travel products and can
earn up to 4% on qualifying bookings. In May 2026, Expedia announced an
AI Activity Planner designed to turn natural-language ideas into personalized,
bookable itineraries. It is also investing in creator-led discovery, direct
customer relationships, B2B distribution, and a broader journey from
inspiration through arrival.

The Lore Club becomes strategically interesting to Expedia if it proves:

- It reaches occasion-based groups Expedia does not efficiently acquire.
- Its plans drive incremental lodging and activity conversion.
- Its group collaboration data improves personalization and attach rates.
- It has a creator network and product workflow deeper than affiliate
  storefronts.
- Its structured itinerary graph can be distributed through Expedia's B2B
  platform or consumer brands.

The pitch is not "we also have creator travel shops." It is:

> We convert proven multi-day group plans into activated trips and attributable
> bookings, with a supply and outcome graph built around occasions.

### Airbnb

Airbnb is a strong product and mission fit, but likely to build generic
planning features internally.

Airbnb's May 2026 release added a shared itinerary, friend travel maps,
AI-assisted discovery, more Experiences, services, transport partnerships, and
boutique hotels. Airbnb reported that almost half of Q4 2025 Experience
bookings were not attached to accommodation bookings, showing that
non-accommodation trip demand can bring new customers to its platform.

The Lore Club becomes strategically interesting to Airbnb if it owns:

- Exclusive or unusually loyal local and occasion-focused creators
- Verified multi-day trip plans that combine homes, Experiences, and Services
- Strong group invitation and collaboration behavior
- Trust signals based on real completed trips
- Repeat local and travel use beyond accommodation bookings

The Airbnb thesis is:

> The Lore Club supplies the human knowledge and group coordination layer that
> turns Airbnb's homes, Experiences, and Services into complete occasion trips.

### Which buyer is more likely?

Expedia currently has the stronger commercial fit because creator discovery,
bookable itineraries, affiliate economics, and B2B distribution are explicit
parts of its strategy. Airbnb has the stronger brand and product fit around
local expertise and group travel, but it has also built more of the adjacent
functionality itself.

For either company, acquisition becomes more plausible only if The Lore Club
owns a concentrated market, a difficult-to-recreate supply network, and
measurable incremental booking demand.

## Metrics that matter

The north-star metric should be:

> Verified group trips completed from a Lore plan.

Supporting metrics:

### Supply

- Active published plans
- Plans with a verified creator trip
- Percentage refreshed in the last 90 or 180 days
- Time from import to publish
- Creator activation and 90-day retention
- Revenue per active creator

### Demand

- Search-to-listing conversion
- Listing-to-purchase conversion
- Purchase-to-Trip-Workspace activation
- Percentage of trips with invited participants
- Average invited participants
- Trip completion and verified-review rate
- Referral and repeat-purchase rate

### Transaction

- Plan GMV and net revenue
- Booking click-through and confirmed booking attach rate
- Gross booking value influenced
- Revenue per activated trip
- Refund, dispute, fraud, and support-contact rates

### Data advantage

- Structured itinerary items
- Percentage geocoded
- Percentage with a current booking source
- Planned-versus-actual spend coverage
- Verified substitutions and closures
- Completed-trip outcome records

Illustrative proof points that would make strategic conversations more
credible include thousands of completed group trips, hundreds of consistently
earning creators, strong group-invite behavior, meaningful booking attachment,
and repeatable dominance in several occasion-destination combinations. These
are evidence goals, not universal acquisition thresholds.

## What not to build

- A generic AI itinerary generator
- A PDF marketplace with no post-purchase workflow
- An undifferentiated "all travel for everyone" catalog
- A social feed before the transaction and trip loops work
- Seller subscriptions before creators can reliably earn
- Sponsored ranking that weakens buyer trust
- A large manual-concierge operation disguised as software
- Scraped recommendations or creator content without durable usage rights

## Acquisition readiness beyond product

An acquirer will also examine operational quality. Prepare early:

- Clean creator agreements and IP licenses
- Contractor and employee invention-assignment agreements
- Clear rights for photos, videos, maps, and imported content
- Documented privacy consent for group and trip data
- Vendor and affiliate contract compliance
- Auditable payouts, refunds, taxes, and marketplace ledger
- Security controls, incident response, backups, and access reviews
- A checked-in data model and migration history
- Reliable cohort and attribution reporting
- Clean cap table, financial records, and a maintained data room
- APIs and modular services that can be integrated without the entire frontend

## Recommended next decision

Commit the product to one category:

> Proven plans for occasion-based group trips.

Then build the smallest complete loop:

1. Creator imports and publishes a structured trip.
2. Buyer discovers and purchases it.
3. Purchase creates an editable shared Trip Workspace.
4. The group invites, decides, budgets, and books.
5. The completed trip generates a verified review and outcome data.
6. The creator receives transparent earnings and performance data.

That loop is both a desirable standalone business and the beginning of an asset
that a major travel marketplace could rationally buy.
